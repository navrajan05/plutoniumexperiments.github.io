UChicago Researchers Secretly Injected Patients With Plutonium. The University Still Hasn't Apologized.

As part of a series of government experiments, a University of Chicago hospital injected terminally ill patients with lethal amounts of plutonium – then covered it up for 50 years. The university has still admitted no wrongdoing.

Report & Design by Navaneeth Rajan

Chapter I  —  The Motive

A New Element

On December 2, 1942, a team of researchers at UChicago led by Enrico Fermi achieved the first nuclear chain reaction in a squash court at the site of the modern-day Regenstein Library. Chicago Pile-1 was a crude graphite-and-uranium reactor; it had no cooling or shielding, output only half a watt of power, and was a radiation hazard to everyone present.

But the goal was never power generation. Unlike uranium, plutonium cannot be found in nature in any meaningful quantity – it must be manufactured. Until then, weapons-grade plutonium had only ever been produced in minuscule amounts by cyclotrons: the earliest particle accelerators. Fermi and the members of Chicago's Metallurgical Laboratory – the "Met Lab" – were tasked with evaluating the feasibility of using nuclear reactors to recreate the underlying nuclear reactions en masse, producing industrial quantities of plutonium.

Wartime had accelerated research on plutonium to exceptional rates. The element had been discovered less than two years before CP-1 criticality, by physicist Glenn Seaborg at UC Berkeley. It was his team that, in August 1942, isolated and weighed the element for the first time, in Room 405 of UChicago's Jones Laboratory.

By early 1943, the U.S. federal government had authorized the purchase of a site in Hanford, Washington for the construction of the world's first plutonium production reactor. Across the country, researchers and site workers were handling the element daily, frequently with inadequate protection. By 1944, there had been several inhalation incidents, and, at Los Alamos, even a case of ingestion. But the biological effects of internal plutonium exposure remained almost entirely unknown.

The Manhattan Project needed answers, and quickly. If a particle of plutonium was breathed in or swallowed, what would happen? How much would the body retain? How quickly would it clear? Without that data, there would be no defensible way to set safety limits for the tens of thousands of workers and researchers building the bomb.

Compared to the groundbreaking research conducted by Seaborg and Fermi, the solution approved by the Manhattan Project Medical Division was remarkably primitive: inject hospital patients with plutonium and measure how much they excreted. Three of the patients were drawn from the wards of Billings Hospital, a two-minute walk from the center of the main quadrangle.

These patients — designated CHI-1, CHI-2, and CHI-3 — were injected in 1945. None were told what was in the syringe. All had been admitted for unrelated conditions. Their excretions were collected and sent to Los Alamos for analysis.

The Safety Limit

Uneducated Guesses

In 1943, the Manhattan Project’s Health Division tentatively set the maximum permissible body burden (MPBB) for plutonium at 5 micrograms. The estimate was loosely extrapolated from radium, a better-studied radioactive heavy metal that, like plutonium, had a tendency to accumulate in the skeleton. By the spring of 1945, it had been discovered that plutonium deposited in parts of the bone more closely associated with growth, and the MPBB was reduced to a single microgram. Following the 1949 Chalk River Conference, it was slashed even further: to 0.1 micrograms — fifty times below the original estimate.

That single green dot is the entire MPBB. One tenth of a microgram. This is what the Health Division ultimately concluded a human being could safely carry.

Patient CHI-1 — April 1945

UChicago's First Injection

The Albert Merritt Billings Hospital was the first hospital to be affiliated with UChicago Medicine, situated roughly five hundred yards southwest of the Stagg Field site where CP-1 had gone critical two and a half years earlier. The plutonium injected into its patients was likely produced at Met Lab facilities on the same campus.

The first Billings patient, Arthur Hubbard, "CHI-1", was a 68-year-old undergoing treatment for cancers of the lung and chin. He was injected with 6.5 micrograms in April 1945. Against even the most permissive 1943 safety limit, this was an overdose. Against the final standard, it was 65 times the safe quantity — each orange dot is more plutonium than any worker at the Met Lab or any other facility was permitted to accumulate.

He died five months later. The experiment continued.

Patients CHI-2 & CHI-3 — December 1945

Even More.

Japan had surrendered in August. The Met Lab was winding down its wartime role. But the Health Division's agenda — to establish reliable data on plutonium excretion — remained incomplete. In December 1945, with no military urgency justifying the work and the MPBB now set at a microgram, two more Billings patients received approximately 95 micrograms each.

Una Macke, "CHI-2", died 17 days later, having been exposed to 25 times the amount of radiation the average person receives over their lifetime. CHI-3, approximately 18 years old, left Billings and vanished from the record entirely. He was reported as "lost to follow-up."

The Calculation

They Were Already Dead

Plutonium accumulates on the surfaces of the bone; over the course of decades, the principal long-term health risk is bone cancer. This much was understood even in 1945, working off the precedent set by the "radium girls." These women, who had licked their brushes as they daubed radioactive paint onto watch dials, had, ten to twenty years later, developed anemia, jaw necrosis, and bone tumors that, for many, proved fatal.

To quote an internal 1946 report, "people were selected whose life expectancy was such that they could not be endangered by injections of plutonium." UChicago researchers chose consciously and explicitly to inject terminally ill patients because they would die of their existing conditions before the plutonium could produce a tumor. That way, there would be no delayed suffering. More importantly, there would be no causal link between injection and death, nor any lawsuit linking a Billings Hospital bed to the Manhattan Project researchers working a few buildings away.

Chicago Was Not the Only Site

The three Billings Hospital patients were part of a larger program. Between 1945 and 1947, eighteen hospitalized patients at four American institutions received plutonium injections. Many of these sites declined to select terminally ill patients as their experimental subjects – others misdiagnosed patients with years, if not decades, of life left. Across all four cohorts and eighteen patients, no record of consent exists.

The first injection of the program did not occur at UChicago. Ebb Cade, a 53-year-old Black construction worker, was admitted to Oak Ridge Hospital following a car accident. Reports described him as "well developed [and] well nourished," and made explicit note of the fact that he had no history of illness. On April 10, 1945, two weeks after the initial crash, researchers injected him with 4.7 micrograms of plutonium. Five days later, they biopsied Cade's bones and removed fifteen of his teeth for plutonium uptake analysis – purportedly, Cade was suffering from tooth decay, though the medical necessity of the procedure is disputed. Cade was retroactively designated as HP-12 (an abbreviation for the sickeningly clinical "human product"). He lived another eight years before dying of heart failure.

At UC San Francisco, "CAL-2" – a four-year-old named Simeon Shaw – was injected with not only plutonium, but also a cocktail of yttrium and cerium radioisotopes. Researchers lied to his parents and told them it was an experimental treatment for Shaw's cancer. He died less than a year later. He had been preceded by Albert Stevens, "CAL-1," who was injected with 131,000 nanocuries of plutonium following a misdiagnosis of a benign gastric ulcer as stomach cancer. He lived for 21 years, accumulating 64 Sieverts of radiation, and becoming the most irradiated human in history.

Chapter III  —  The Defense

The Dose Makes the Poison

In February 1995, responding to a declassification order issued by Secretary of Energy Hazel O'Leary, the University of California, San Francisco released its own internal review of the plutonium experiments. The report of the UCSF Ad Hoc Fact-Finding Committee on World War II Human Radiation Experiments concluded that "the amount of plutonium injected into the CAL patients was too small to pose a significant risk of physical harm," arguing that "no UCSF research subject received more than 0.4% of the mass noted to produce a detectable acute effect" in animal studies.

Strictly speaking, the claim is true. But the defense built upon that premise is both historically fraught and medically irrelevant.

The Historical Problem

The Researchers Couldn't Have Known That in 1945

The "acutely toxic dose" that UCSF cites was derived from early LD50 studies on rats: experiments establishing how much plutonium kills half a test population within a month. But what few animal studies existed in April 1945 on the toxicity of plutonium were already widely understood to be unlikely to generalize to humans. The historical record for plutonium safety limits proves this – by 1949, the limit was set at a fiftieth of what it had been when the rat LD50 experiments were first conducted. UCSF's report was, in effect, citing data that it acknowledged as faulty, in order to defend decisions made in 1945 by researchers who also understood that the data was faulty. Nobody knew what an acutely toxic dose looked like, because nobody had done the experiments yet.

The Biological Problem

Acute Toxicity Is the Wrong Metric Entirely

Acute toxicity, by definition, refers to adverse health effects suffered within two weeks of exposure to a toxin. At microgram-order doses, plutonium does nothing quickly. Its hazard is not acute poisoning — it is radiation-induced osteosarcoma, developing over the span of decades. The underlying biochemical mechanisms were understood by analogy to radium well before the first human injections, and the existing safety limits — the MPBB of 1 microgram, later 0.1 — were set explicitly to mitigate cancer risk, not because anyone expected to kill patients in a month. To defend a dose as safe because it is not acutely toxic is, thus, to answer a question nobody was asking.

A Different Time

While the UCSF report does underplay the health risks posed by the injections themselves, it does concede that the experimental design itself was monstrously unethical – describing it as "comparable to research performed in Nazi death camps." This indictment was not a universal one: a report by Los Alamos National Laboratory, published in the same year as UCSF's, defended the procedure, claiming "it was not uncommon in the 1940s for physicians to use patients as subjects in experiments without their knowledge or consent."

Report CH-3607 — titled "The Distribution and Excretion of Plutonium in Two Human Subjects" — was briefly declassified on January 3, 1947. It was reclassified eighty-seven days later, on March 31, 1947. The following is what the record shows about why.

Col. O.G. Haywood
It is desired that no document be released which refers to experiments with humans and might have adverse effects on public opinion or result in legal suits.
C.L. Marshall
This document appears to be the most dangerous since it describes experiments performed on human subjects... the coldly scientific manner in which the results are tabulated and discussed would have a very poor effect on the general public.
J.C. Franklin
Publication of experiments done by Atomic Energy Commission contractors' personnel may frequently be the source of litigation and prejudicial to the proper functioning of the Atomic Energy Commission Insurance Branch.
Dr. R.S. Stone
With regard to the lawsuit part of the program, I think that this could be taken care of readily by the elimination of the initials of the patients... With the initials removed, there will be no means by which the patients can ever connect themselves with the report.

The stated rationale for the classification, consistent across every memo in the chain, was the same: not weapons secrets, nor national security, but public outrage and civil liability. Even the researchers knew the work was unconscionable: in 1950, Dr. Joseph Hamilton — who had personally supervised the California injections — wrote that the human radiation experiments carried "a little of the Buchenwald touch." At the Buchenwald concentration camp, Nazi doctors infected over a thousand Jewish and homosexual inmates with typhus, gas gangrene, and smallpox, among other diseases. Most met sickening deaths.

Epilogue

What Remains Unsaid

In January 1994 — one month after Secretary of Energy Hazel O'Leary ordered the declassification of Manhattan Project human experiment records — the University of Chicago released the conservatively titled "Statement on Plutonium Experiments." It was the institution's first public acknowledgment of the research. No press conference accompanied it. No spokesperson took questions. The statement was released in response to congressional inquiry, not institutional initiative. What follows are the final two lines, and a brief analysis of what they do and do not say.

Excerpt - Statement on Plutonium Experiments
"A follow-up study found no evidence that the plutonium injections affected the health of the subjects or their pre-existing illnesses.

Research standards have changed drastically since 1945. Today, no research is or can be performed on any patients without their full knowledge and informed, written consent."
Released in response to the ACHRE investigation. No admission of wrongdoing accompanied the statement.

The University of Chicago is not the only institution with a history of unethical experimentation. Vanderbilt irradiated pregnant women with iron radioisotopes. MIT researchers fed children with developmental disabilities oatmeal and milk enriched with radioactive iron and calcium. Unlike UChicago, both institutions have since apologized.

More saliently, Vanderbilt is not where plutonium was first isolated, as the plaques in the lobby of the Jones Chemical Laboratory are happy to remind you. MIT is not the parent institution of Argonne National Laboratory. UChicago is the only university where touring prospies are shepherded to the site of the world's first nuclear reactor, invited to take photos of the statue commemorating the event. It is unabashedly proud of its role in the history of nuclear research, more so, perhaps, than any other university (any university except Berkeley, at least). Is it permissible that it obscures its role in the more sordid parts of that history?
